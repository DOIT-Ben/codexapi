Abstract

Temporal action localization aims to identify action instances in
untrimmed videos and predict their temporal boundaries. Existing
Transformer-based methods improve temporal dependency modeling, but
stacked attention aggregation may smooth short-range variations and
weaken discriminative cues near ambiguous action boundaries. To address
this problem, we propose SAEFormer, a representation-level cue
enhancement framework for multi-scale temporal action localization. In
this paper, the core of SAEFormer is SAEBlock, which consists of
Learnable Multi-branch Semantic Fusion (LMSF) and Boundary-aware
Enhancement (BAE). LMSF mitigates the smoothing effect on local
discriminative cues around attention aggregation while preserving
contextual information. BAE enhances transition-sensitive temporal
representation cues by residually reweighting boundary-related temporal
positions, and learns boundary-oriented reweighting without introducing
an additional boundary-level loss.

Experiments on three challenging benchmarks validate the competitiveness
of SAEFormer. It achieves 69.7% and 71.4% average mAP on THUMOS14 under
two feature settings, 36.7% on ActivityNet-1.3 under the main setting,
and 26.5% / 23.7% for EPIC-KITCHENS-100 verb / noun localization.
Ablation studies further show that LMSF and BAE provide complementary
gains.

Keywords: Temporal action localization, Untrimmed video understanding,
Semantic-aware enhancement, Boundary-oriented reweighting

Introduction

Temporal action localization (TAL) is an important task in long-form
intelligent video understanding. It aims to identify action instances in
untrimmed videos and estimate their start and end timestamps [1,2],
supporting applications such as video content analysis, multimedia
retrieval, sports analysis, and intelligent surveillance [3-5]. In these
scenarios, TAL converts weakly structured video streams into temporally
localized action segments, providing structured evidence for retrieval,
monitoring, and downstream decision-making. Unlike trimmed action
recognition, TAL must reason over long temporal sequences that usually
involve large variations in action duration, ambiguous action-background
transitions [6], and a high proportion of background content. Therefore,
TAL is sensitive not only to semantic recognition errors but also to
small boundary shifts, because such shifts affect detection quality and
downstream decision support under strict temporal
intersection-over-union thresholds.

Existing TAL methods have evolved from convolutional and
graph-structured architectures to Transformer-based frameworks [7-11].
Convolutional models provide useful local temporal inductive biases,
whereas graph-structured designs model relations among clips or
proposals. Transformer-based methods further apply attention along the
temporal dimension to improve long-range temporal dependency modeling.
In particular, ActionFormer [10] establishes a strong point-based
baseline by combining local self-attention with multi-scale feature
pyramids. This line of work has made attention-based temporal modeling a
widely adopted direction in one-stage TAL. However, when multiple
modules are stacked, attention-based context aggregation may also reduce
local temporal contrast. It is therefore necessary to perform
representation-level cue enhancement to complement the attention
mechanism rather than replace the detection framework.

This problem is particularly prominent in background-dominated videos.
Attention aggregation helps exchange contextual information, but it may
smooth short-range temporal variations and weaken differences between
adjacent action and background clips. When multiple temporal modeling
modules are stacked, this effect can be further amplified. Related
studies on vision Transformers also suggest that self-attention may
cause patch or token representations to converge or become over-smoothed
[12,13]. In long-sequence TAL modeling, a similar tendency may further
weaken boundary-sensitive local variations. As a result, a model may
capture global context while still losing part of the local evidence
needed to distinguish action starts, action interiors, and action ends.

This observation raises two representation-level questions: how to
preserve local discriminative cues during attention-based context
aggregation, and how to enhance boundary-oriented temporal transitions
without introducing additional boundary supervision. We argue that
effective temporal localization requires representation-level cues with
different roles. Local saliency and identity information help preserve
short-range temporal contrast, convolutional modulation provides local
context, and boundary-oriented temporal responses emphasize
transition-sensitive positions. Therefore, we aim to organize these cues
inside the temporal backbone, without replacing the attention-based
backbone modeling paradigm or relying on additional boundary
supervision.

To address this problem, we propose SAEFormer, a one-stage, multi-scale,
point-based framework for temporal action localization. In this paper,
semantic-aware enhancement refers to representation-level cue
enhancement rather than external semantic reasoning. SAEBlock preserves
local discriminative cues before and after attention aggregation through
lightweight learnable cue fusion. BAE learns boundary-oriented temporal
reweighting through the standard temporal action localization objective,
without adding boundary-level supervision.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

We propose SAEBlock, in which LMSF mitigates the smoothing effect of
stacked temporal attention on local discriminative cues during context
aggregation, thereby improving the discriminability of temporal
representations near boundaries.

We introduce BAE, which enhances transition-sensitive temporal
representation cues by residually reweighting boundary-related temporal
positions, without requiring additional boundary supervision.

We conduct extensive experiments on multiple mainstream datasets and
achieve competitive performance, while systematic ablations on THUMOS14
validate the effectiveness of the proposed modules.

Related Work

Temporal Action Localization (TAL)

Temporal action localization (TAL) aims to locate and classify action
instances in untrimmed videos. Existing TAL methods are commonly divided
into two-stage and one-stage frameworks [14]. Two-stage methods such as
BMN [15] and G-TAD [16] follow a proposal-then-classify paradigm, where
candidate temporal segments are first generated and then classified.
This design benefits from explicit proposal modeling, but the separation
between proposal generation and classification may introduce error
propagation and increase inference latency.

One-stage methods reduce pipeline complexity by directly predicting
temporal boundaries and action categories. CNN-based methods [17-19]
improve end-to-end detection efficiency, while anchor-free methods such
as AFSD [8] and DyFADet [20] further remove hand-crafted anchor design
and shift toward direct boundary regression. More recent
Transformer-based baselines, including RTD-Net [21], ActionFormer [10],
and TallFormer [22], strengthen long-range temporal dependencies and
have become strong reference frameworks. These developments reduce
pipeline complexity, but long videos with variable action durations and
background-dominated content still place high demands on reliable
point-level classification and boundary regression.

Temporal Context Modeling

Effective temporal context modeling is essential for TAL because action
instances vary substantially in duration and are often surrounded by
background segments. Previous studies have explored convolution-based,
graph-structured, and Transformer-based strategies [9,10,23]. CNN-based
methods such as TCANet [17] and ContextLoc [18] aggregate
multi-granularity temporal contexts, whereas graph-structured designs
such as G-TAD [16] model relations among clips or proposals. These
designs improve temporal dependency modeling, but their ability to
jointly represent local boundary variations and long-range context
remains limited in complex untrimmed videos.

Recent studies have therefore introduced more adaptive or
boundary-sensitive temporal modeling mechanisms. TriDet [24] replaces
self-attention with convolutions adapted to different temporal scales,
modeling local context at multiple granularities for point-based
detection. TemporalMaxer [25] revisits max-pooling-based context
aggregation, and CTAN [26] combines convolution with Transformer-style
modeling to preserve fine-grained local variations. DyFADet [20]
adaptively adjusts receptive fields and convolutional weights for
actions of different durations, while a diffusion-based method [27]
introduces generative denoising diffusion to improve temporal action
detection. Related methods such as ASL [28], TE-TAD [29], DualH [19],
and BRTAL [30] further emphasize the importance of action-sensitive
representation learning, time-aligned detection, hierarchical temporal
modeling, and boundary refinement. These studies mainly modify temporal
operators, detection heads, or refinement mechanisms. How to preserve
cue diversity inside attention blocks remains less directly discussed.

Transformer-Based TAL Methods

Transformers have achieved strong performance in sequence modeling [23]
and have been widely adopted in temporal action localization.
ActionFormer [10] integrates local self-attention with a feature pyramid
and establishes a strong point-based Transformer baseline. TallFormer
[22] introduces a long-memory mechanism to reduce the cost of processing
high-resolution long videos. TeST [31] adopts a temporal-spatial
separated Transformer architecture to jointly model spatial and temporal
cues, and DiGIT [32] advances query-based Transformer detection through
a multi-dilated gated encoder and a central-adjacent decoder. These
methods mainly improve temporal dependency modeling and decoding
strategies over long video sequences.

Other studies extend representation learning and training paradigms for
Transformer-based temporal action localization from complementary
perspectives. ADS [11] introduces a dual selective token-mixing
mechanism to suppress redundant information and enhance discriminative
boundary representations. TFFormer [33] performs cross time-frequency
interaction to capture local temporal details and global frequency
patterns. AdaTAD [34] explores end-to-end temporal action localization
based on large-scale VideoMAE, and Dual DETRs [35] use instance-level
and boundary-level queries for multi-label TAD. In parallel with these
Transformer-based directions, State-Sensitive Mamba [36] advances
temporal localization from the perspective of state-space long-sequence
modeling. Different from methods that mainly improve temporal operators,
query decoders, or training paradigms, SAEFormer starts from
representation enhancement inside the Transformer backbone. It uses LMSF
to mitigate the smoothing effect of attention aggregation on local
discriminative cues and BAE to perform boundary-oriented reweighting on
boundary-related temporal positions. In the full configuration, these
two modules jointly form SAEBlock and improve the discriminability of
representations near boundaries.

Methodology

As shown in Fig. 1, SAEFormer takes pre-extracted clip-level features as
input. After embedding projection, the features enter a temporal
encoding backbone stacked with SAEBlocks. In this paper, "semantic"
specifically refers to action-related discriminative representation cues
encoded by pre-trained video features, including action-background
discrimination cues, local temporal variation cues, and boundary
transition cues. It does not refer to external language semantics,
category-name semantics, or knowledge-based reasoning semantics. In the
full configuration, SAEBlock consists of Learnable Multi-branch Semantic
Fusion (LMSF) and BAE. The two modules can also be ablated as separable
components, where LMSF is responsible for local cue preservation and BAE
for boundary-oriented reweighting. The multi-scale features are then
used by a 1D feature pyramid and point-based classification and boundary
decoding heads to generate predictions, followed by Soft-NMS for
redundancy removal.

Experiments

Experimental Setup

Datasets

We evaluate the proposed SAEFormer on three challenging benchmarks:
THUMOS14 [40], ActivityNet-1.3 [1], and EPIC-KITCHENS-100 [41]. THUMOS14
contains 413 untrimmed videos covering 20 action categories. It is
divided into validation and test subsets, with 200 videos for validation
and 213 videos for testing. ActivityNet-1.3 is a large-scale action
dataset containing 200 activity classes and approximately 20,000 videos,
with a total duration of more than 600 hours. It is divided into three
subsets: 10,024 training videos, 4,926 validation videos, and 5,044 test
videos. EPIC-KITCHENS-100 is a large-scale egocentric action dataset. It
contains 100 hours of video from 700 sessions, recording cooking
activities in different kitchens.

Evaluation Metrics

To evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method, we follow common
evaluation protocols in temporal action localization [10,22,24].
Performance is measured by mean average precision (mAP) under multiple
temporal Intersection over Union (tIoU) thresholds. The tIoU thresholds
are set to [0.3: 0.1: 0.7] for THUMOS14, [0.5: 0.05: 0.95] for
ActivityNet-1.3, and [0.1: 0.1: 0.5] for EPIC-KITCHENS-100. The notation
[start: step: end] denotes a set of thresholds that starts from start,
uses step as the interval, and ends at end. For example, [0.3: 0.1: 0.7]
corresponds to 0.3, 0.4, 0.5, 0.6, and 0.7. The other threshold settings
follow the same notation rule.

Implementation Details

All experiments use pre-extracted clip-level features: THUMOS14 uses I3D
[37] and VideoMAE V2 [42], ActivityNet-1.3 reports results with both I3D
and TSP [43] features, and EPIC-KITCHENS-100 uses SlowFast [38]. Unless
otherwise stated, the ActivityNet implementation details refer to the
main TSP setting. The experiments are implemented in Python 3.10 with
PyTorch 2.10.0 and CUDA 12.8, and are run on two NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPUs.
We train for 54, 50, 17, 30, and 33 epochs on THUMOS14-I3D,
THUMOS14-VideoMAE V2, ActivityNet-1.3, EPIC-KITCHENS-100 noun, and
EPIC-KITCHENS-100 verb, respectively, with 24, 20, 5, 7, and 10 warm-up
epochs. The optimizer uses cosine annealing [44] together with AdamW
[45]. The learning rate is 1e-4 for THUMOS14 and EPIC-KITCHENS-100, and
1e-3 for ActivityNet-1.3. The batch sizes for THUMOS14-I3D,
THUMOS14-VideoMAE V2, ActivityNet-1.3, EPIC-KITCHENS-100 noun, and
EPIC-KITCHENS-100 verb are 2, 2, 16, 2, and 2, respectively.

In addition to the above settings, we follow point-level label
assignment, center sampling, regression range settings, candidate
filtering, and Soft-NMS post-processing protocols consistent with the
ActionFormer-style baseline. Because different datasets and feature
settings have different temporal resolutions and action-duration
distributions, the local window, MLP dimension, number of candidates,
and NMS parameters are set according to the corresponding dataset
configuration. Within the same dataset, the main model and ablation
experiments use consistent training, inference, and evaluation
protocols.

THUMOS14

On THUMOS14, SAEFormer achieves average mAP scores of 69.7% and 71.4%
under the I3D and VideoMAE V2 feature settings, respectively, obtaining
the best average results among the publicly reported results listed in
Table 1. Specifically, under the I3D setting, SAEFormer obtains a small
advantage over recent methods. Under the VideoMAE V2 setting, SAEFormer
achieves strong results in average mAP and at most tIoU thresholds, but
it does not surpass existing methods at every threshold.

For the VideoMAE V2 comparison, we use the VideoMAE V2 model of Wang et
al. [42] to extract features and report it separately from I3D-based
comparisons to avoid direct confusion between different feature
settings. Because the number of publicly reported same-feature VideoMAE
V2 results is still limited, this setting is mainly used to show the
performance of SAEFormer when combined with stronger video
representations, rather than to provide a complete ranking of all
methods under unified features.

is class-agnostic, with only one foreground action class in the
detection head. As a result, category-discriminative cues contribute
less to the final metric, and performance depends more on proposal
quality and long-range temporal coverage. Therefore, local boundary cue
enhancement alone brings more limited gains on this benchmark.

EPIC-KITCHENS-100

On EPIC-KITCHENS-100, SAEFormer achieves 26.5% average mAP for verb
localization and 23.7% for noun localization, as shown in Table 3. These
results are consistent with the fine-grained and egocentric nature of
EPIC-KITCHENS-100, where action instances are defined by verb-noun
interactions in untrimmed first-person videos. Compared with
activity-level benchmarks such as ActivityNet, verb localization in
EPIC-KITCHENS-100 is more directly associated with hand motion dynamics,
temporal transition patterns, and start-end boundary changes. These
factors are aligned with the design of SAEFormer, which enhances local
temporal cues and boundary-oriented representation cues. In contrast,
noun localization depends more heavily on fine-grained object appearance
and hand-object interactions. Since SAEFormer does not explicitly
introduce object-centric modeling, the noun result should be interpreted
as competitive rather than consistently superior to the strongest noun
baseline.

Ablation Study

We conduct ablation studies on THUMOS14 to examine the contributions of
the proposed components. Since the main manuscript focuses on the
primary effectiveness of LMSF and BAE, we report the main component
analysis under I3D features in Table 4. Additional ablations, including
the VideoMAE V2 setting, internal variants of LMSF and BAE, and the
transfer of BAE to ActionFormer, are provided in the supplementary
material. Specifically, Tables S1-S4 report additional component and
variant analyses, Table S5 provides diagnostic error analysis, Table S6
reports model complexity, and Figs. S1-S3 provide qualitative
localization and visualization analyses.

As shown in Table 4, LMSF improves the average mAP from 67.89 to 69.16,
and BAE alone improves it to 69.18. The full model further improves the
average mAP to 69.70, achieving the best average performance among the
listed variants. These results suggest that LMSF and BAE provide
complementary effects in average localization performance. However, the
improvements are not monotonically additive at every tIoU threshold. For
example, LMSF alone obtains a slightly higher mAP at tIoU 0.5, while BAE
alone obtains a higher mAP at tIoU 0.7. Therefore, we interpret the two
components as complementary in terms of average localization performance
rather than consistently superior at every individual threshold.

Conclusion

We propose SAEFormer, a semantic-aware representation enhancement
framework for temporal action localization. Instead of redesigning the
detection pipeline, SAEFormer strengthens temporal representations
through LMSF and BAE. LMSF preserves local discriminative cues during
stacked temporal attention, while BAE provides boundary-oriented
residual reweighting without explicit boundary-level supervision.

The experiments show that SAEFormer is most effective in
boundary-sensitive, multi-category action localization scenarios, where
temporal transitions, action-background discrimination, and
category-discriminative cues are important. Its gains are more limited
in class-agnostic proposal-oriented localization and in object-centric
noun localization, where performance depends more on long-range proposal
quality, object appearance, and hand-object interactions. These results
indicate that local temporal and boundary cue enhancement is useful but
not sufficient for all TAL settings. Future work will explore adaptive
cue weighting, object-aware representations, and more detailed boundary
response analysis.
