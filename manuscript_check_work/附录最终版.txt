Supplementary Material for "SAEFormer: Semantic-Aware Enhancement for
Temporal Action Localization"

This supplementary material provides additional ablation studies,
complexity analysis, visualization analysis, and diagnostic analyses
referenced in the main manuscript. Tables S1-S4 report additional
ablations; Table S5 reports diagnostic error analysis; Table S6 reports
model complexity; and Figs. S1-S3 report qualitative and visualization
analyses. These analyses support the component effectiveness and
interpretation of LMSF and BAE rather than introducing additional claims
beyond the main manuscript.

S1. Additional Ablation Studies

Tables S1-S4 report additional ablations on THUMOS14. They cover the
main component analysis under VideoMAE V2 features, LMSF design
variants, BAE design variants, and the transfer of BAE to ActionFormer.
Unless otherwise specified, LMSF and BAE are treated as detachable
components in the supplementary ablations; the complete SAEBlock
corresponds to enabling both components.

Table S3 evaluates the boundary modeling design under the matched
plain-block protocol. LMSF is disabled for all rows. BAE-diff denotes a
fixed-difference variant that keeps the channel calibration step but
replaces the learnable temporal transition response branch with a
hand-crafted first-order temporal difference operation. Specifically, it
builds a one-dimensional temporal semantic sequence from channel-wise
average and maximum responses with fixed equal weights, and then uses
positive first-order differences to generate the boundary reweighting
map. This variant is used to examine whether fixed temporal gradient
cues are sufficient for boundary-oriented reweighting. Full BAE improves
the average mAP under both I3D and VideoMAE V2 features, whereas
BAE-diff is weak or unstable, suggesting that BAE mainly benefits from
data-adaptive temporal reweighting responses learned under the TAL
objective.

than as a claim of backbone-independent generalization.

S2. Diagnostic Error Analysis

Table S5 provides a diagnostic error analysis on THUMOS14 using I3D
features. Avg. mAP is computed over tIoU thresholds from 0.3 to 0.7 with
a step of 0.1, while average boundary error and Top-1000 precision are
evaluated at tIoU = 0.5. Boundary error is computed over class-aware,
one-to-one matched prediction-ground-truth pairs. For each matched pair,
the boundary error is defined as [], where [] and [] denote the
predicted start and end timestamps, and [] and [] denote the
corresponding ground-truth timestamps. The reported value is averaged
over all matched pairs and measured in seconds. Top-1000 precision is
computed from the globally top-ranked 1,000 predictions after
post-processing, including NMS, where a true positive must match an
unmatched ground-truth instance of the same class.

The full SAEBlock configuration achieves the highest Avg. mAP and
Top-1000 precision, and the lowest average boundary error among the
listed variants. Notably, BAE alone does not consistently improve all
diagnostic measures. Therefore, this analysis supports the combined
LMSF+BAE design rather than serving as standalone causal evidence for
BAE alone or as proof of an explicit boundary-supervision mechanism.

S5. Visualization Analysis

S5.1 Temporal Feature Similarity Analysis

To further understand the effect of LMSF, we visualize temporal feature
similarity at different processing stages. Specifically, we compute
pairwise cosine similarities among temporal features before MHSA, after
MHSA, and after the post-fusion output of LMSF. As shown in Fig. S2, the
features before MHSA retain structured temporal similarity patterns.
After MHSA, the similarity matrix exhibits stronger global similarity,
suggesting that attention aggregation may smooth local temporal contrast
and reduce discriminative differences among temporal positions. After
LMSF, the heatmap shows a more structured similarity distribution,
indicating that part of the local temporal contrast is preserved after
cue fusion. This qualitative observation is consistent with the role of
LMSF in mitigating the smoothing of local discriminative cues and
preserving temporal discriminability. However, it should be interpreted
as visualization-based supporting evidence rather than direct proof of
an explicit boundary mechanism.

[]

Fig. S2. Temporal feature similarity across different processing stages
of SAEBlock. The heatmaps show pairwise cosine similarities among
temporal features before MHSA, after MHSA, and after the post-fusion
output of LMSF. MHSA denotes Multi-Head Self-Attention, and LMSF denotes
Learnable Multi-branch Semantic Fusion.

S5.2 Boundary-Oriented Reweighting Analysis

To further examine the behavior of BAE, we analyze the effective
reweighting gain over different temporal regions. Fig. S3 summarizes the
region-level gains of BAE0-BAE6 under a 10% boundary window. BAE0-BAE6
denote the BAE modules in different SAEBlock layers ordered from shallow
to deep along the forward path. Here, 10% denotes a temporal
neighborhood around each ground-truth start and end boundary with a
width equal to 10% of the corresponding action instance length, and this
window is used to compute the average reweighting gain near boundaries.
As shown in Fig. S3(a), later BAE layers tend to assign larger gains to
boundary neighborhoods. Fig. S3(b) further reports the relative gain
ratios between boundary regions and action interiors/background regions.
BAE5 shows a relatively balanced boundary-oriented gain pattern, while
BAE6 produces the strongest relative boundary emphasis. These
observations provide diagnostic evidence that BAE can learn
boundary-oriented temporal reweighting under the TAL objective, rather
than relying on explicit boundary-level supervision.

[]

Fig. S3. Boundary-oriented reweighting analysis across BAE layers. (a)
Region-level gains for boundary, action-interior, and background
regions. (b) Relative gain ratios between boundary regions and action
interiors/background regions. The shaded area highlights BAE5, and the
dashed line denotes equal gain.
